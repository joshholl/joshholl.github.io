Storytime!
----------

Early in my career I was hell bent on learning Linux because, of course to my youngerself, Linux was
far superior. However, as much as I wanted to I could not escape the grasp of windows. The problem
wasn't that Windows was superior, instead I had become lazy and much like a bolt, rust had settled
in my threads making movement labored. Eventually, I broke out the heavy tools and broke free of the
oxidation that had frozen my learning in place. I knew I had to do something drastic, afterall I am a
very binary person in nature, that is I either do something or I don't. I had to just switch, full
time, no running Linux or dual booting. Just. Switch.

So I did it. I set about copying all of my important files over to my Zip Drive while the iso for
Slackware 8.1 trickled in over my 56k modem line. By then end of the night the bandaid had been
swiftly removed and in place of my comfortable Windows XP install was my new Linux system. It may
have taken a few tries, with pain, agony, and retries along the way. But it was done. And it was
foreign and weird and I hated every bit of it. It wouldn't be  another two weeks before I
would use my PC for anything outside of checking email. But as time progressed I got used to this
new world. I learned the nuances of KDE and compiling software myself and learning how to use the
packaging tools inside of Slackware. I began to love it.

History repeats itself
----------------------

Fast forward 14 or so years to today where I have embraced Linux but fallen In love with OSX. While
somehow making a career as a Windows .net developer. I remember what I have learned in Linux and
cans still quite comfortably navigate my way around. While I have picked up some command line
knowledge over the years I remain far from a command line guru that can replace anyone with a small
shellscript, no matter how intense my desires maybe! And that is exactly what needs to change. Not
that I would like to replace anyone with a shellscript but, hey, having options is nice.

However something has been holding me back from gaining intimate knowledge of the command line.I have
never been able to go past they awkward I-wish-I-could-just-use-my-mouse stage. Perhaps because I
became ever dependent on Visual Studio and Resharper. Maybe it was still a fight or flight reponse
from my initial trauma at the hands of the Slackware install process. Or maybe, as it had been with 
learning Linux, pure, unadulterated laziness. Who knows?

What I do know is this: over the past three months I have been working on front end applications
written in ES6. Given that Visual Studio is a bit hefty to say the least, and the version that we
use doesn't have the best ES6 support the team I am on decided to use Atom for our development. I've
had to break the ties the IDE I was comfortable with, the tooling I have grown to love, and forced
into a land of powershell. I am now ready, now is the time for me to bite the bullet, put on my big
boy pants and learn to be an effecitve and knowledgable command line user.

Where to now?
------------

Thats a good question! Honestly I dont know yet. But, I have a plan; a drastic one to boot! I bought
a Chromebook. And while you may ask what the hell does a purely visual OS have to do with learning
command line, I think the answer will eventually be everything. I think part of my laziness is the
power of the machines I have. Here I am, writing this post on a Core i7 MacBook Pro with 16GB of ram
and a large SSD hard drive. While I certainly love my laptop, I have nothing that motivates me to use
lightweight tools like Vim, although that happens to be what I am using. Once the Chromebook arrives
I plan on hooking my trusty mbp up to the lovely display in my office and leaving it there. The
Chromebook with be violated and end up with [https://github.com/dnschneid/crouton Crouton] installed
on it. I will be placed in my backpack and become my every day pc.
